# Reference — How Roman, Viking & English boats were built (plank by plank)

Deep-research report (23 confirmed findings, 3-vote adversarial verification; 2 claims killed). For the **Build mode** ship builder: real construction sequence + a game translation. Sources at the bottom.

## The big picture: two axes

Every tradition here sits on **two independent axes**, which is exactly what makes them distinct *buildable systems*:

1. **Shell-first vs frame-first** — is the hull's shape/strength in the **planking** (build the skin first, drop frames in after) or in the **skeleton** (raise frames first, then clad them)?
2. **Clinker vs carvel** — do the planks **overlap** (lapstrake, riveted plank-to-plank) or sit **flush** edge-to-edge (fastened to frames / edge-joined)?

|  | Shell-first | Frame-first |
|---|---|---|
| **Clinker (overlap + rivets)** | **Viking** | — |
| **Carvel (flush)** | **Roman** (edge-joined by mortise-&-tenon) | **English Age-of-Sail** (planks on frames, treenails) |

The one-line story: **Roman = shell-first flush (M&T)** → over the mid-1st millennium AD the Mediterranean shifted to **frame-first carvel** → medieval **England was clinker (Viking-descended)** → **c.1500 (Mary Rose) England adopts the southern frame-first carvel method**, matured by ~1710 into the classic ship-of-the-line build.

## 1. Roman / classical Mediterranean — shell-first, carvel, mortise-and-tenon

**Sequence:** keel → stem + sternpost → **garboard strake**, then strakes built **up from the keel, flush** → for every plank seam the shell **self-locks** (see joinery) → *only once the shell is rigid* are **frames dropped in and fastened** → deck beams, mast step.

**Joinery (the signature):** adjacent planks are edge-joined **to each other, not to frames**, by **pegged mortise-and-tenon**: cut a mortise into each facing plank edge, insert a free **tenon**, then drive a **locking peg/dowel** through holes drilled through both the mortise wall and the tenon. Joints are closely spaced (~120 mm) and **structural** — engineering studies (FE / ultimate-strength) confirm they carry load, they're not just alignment aids. The planking shell alone holds the hull's shape before any frame exists.

*Note:* late-Roman builds began un-pegging the tenons and spacing them wider as frames took the load — the start of the shell→skeleton transition. The classic **pegged** M&T is the iconic version. Warship (bireme/trireme) construction is *inferred* from merchant wrecks + the *Olympias* reconstruction — no trireme wreck has ever been found.

## 2. Viking / Norse — shell-first, clinker (lapstrake), iron rivets + roves

**Sequence** (Viking Ship Museum, Roskilde — the reconstruction authority): make the **T-shaped oak keel** + **stem** + **sternpost** first (the "backbone", joined by **scarf joints**, 3 iron rivets each, tarred wool caulking in the scarf) → fasten the **garboard strake** to the keel → rivet each next strake so it **overlaps the one below on the outside** → after several strakes, **insert the frames/ribs**, the lower ones **lashed to cleats** carved into the already-fitted planks → continue upper strakes, add **crossbeams** and the **kerling / mast-fish** (mast step); caulk laps with **tarred wool / animal hair**.

**Joinery (the signature):** a **two-part iron rivet-and-rove** clench. Drill a pilot hole through the overlap; drive a pointed **rivet** from **outside** (backed by a dolly); set a loose square iron **rove** (washer) over the shank **inside**; cut the excess; cold-**peen** the stub flat over the rove. The **plank-to-plank rivets ARE the structure** — frames are secondary and go in last. (Upper strakes also secured to frames/knees with wooden **treenails**.) Archaeologically nailed down by Gokstad (~900) & Oseberg (~820): frames lashed to cleats prove **planking preceded framing**.

## 3. English — clinker cog → Mary Rose transition → Age-of-Sail frame-first carvel

Medieval English ships were **clinker** (the cog — northern, Viking-descended). Then a **revolution from the early 1500s** (Mary Rose, 1511): England drops clinker for the **Mediterranean/Iberian carvel plank-on-frame** method — flush planks on a **rigid pre-built frame**, with **no direct fastenings between adjacent strakes**. In clinker the plank joins are the structure; in carvel **the frame is the structure and the planks are just skin**.

**Age-of-Sail sequence (~1710, from Sutherland's 1711 *The Ship-builders Assistant*), frame-first:**
1. **Keel** — ELM, up to four pieces scarphed end-to-end (4-ft vertical scarphs, eight ~0.95" iron bolts), **rabbetted** along the sides to receive the garboard.
2. **Stem** (OAK, 2 pieces on a 500-tonner, scarphed on) + **sternpost** (single OAK, **tenoned into a mortice in the keel**) + deadwoods.
3. **Floors** (the principal bottom transverse timbers) — each a **single piece**; bolt **selected** floors to the keel first, run temporary **ribbands** around their heads across the sirmarks to lock the shape, then **infill the remaining floors** at ~21.5" "room and space".
4. **Futtocks + top timbers** raised to the rail → the **rigid skeleton is complete**.
5. **Outer planking, flush (carvel), beginning with the wales**, then the remaining strakes — fastened with **treenails PLUS iron fastenings** (a game should model both, not treenails alone).
6. **Internal structure last:** keelson, deck beams, **knees**, mast step.
7. Caulk all seams with **oakum**.

The keel's **rabbet** receiving the garboard is the carvel analogue of the Viking garboard-to-keel lap — the same structural handshake, solved two ways.

## Game translation → the Build-mode builder

### Piece taxonomy (each a placeable part)
`keel` (backbone; T-profile Viking, rabbetted carvel) · `stem` (bow post) · `sternpost` · `garboard strake` (first plank on the keel) · `strakes/planks` (numbered courses up the hull) · `wales` (thick strakes / first carvel course) · `floors` (bottom frames) · `futtocks` + `top timbers` (upper frame segments) · `ribs/frames` (Viking; added last, some lashed to cleats) · `keelson` (inner spine over the floors) · `deck beams` · `knees` (beam-to-frame brackets) · `mast step / kerling` · fastenings: `treenails` / `rivets+roves` / `pegged tenons` · caulking: `oakum` (carvel) / `tarred wool` (clinker).

### Build ORDER as three distinct gameplay sequences
- **Viking** (shell-first clinker): keel → scarf on stem+sternpost (3 rivets + tarred wool each) → garboard on the keel → **rivet each strake overlapping the last** (per-rivet loop: drill → drive from outside on a dolly → set rove inside → cut → peen) → after ~5 strakes drop in ribs, **lash lower ribs to cleats** → upper strakes + crossbeams + kerling mast-step → caulk laps.
- **Roman** (shell-first carvel-M&T): keel → stem+sternpost → garboard, then **strakes flush up from the keel** → per seam: cut mortises, insert tenons, **drive a locking peg** — the shell self-locks with **no frames yet** → once rigid, drop in frames → deck beams + mast step.
- **English Age-of-Sail** (frame-first carvel): lay elm keel (scarph 4 pieces, bolt) → oak stem scarphed + oak sternpost tenoned into keel + deadwoods → **bolt selected floors, run ribbands, infill floors** (~21.5" spacing) → raise futtocks + top timbers → **rigid skeleton** → **plank flush beginning with the wales**, treenails → keelson, deck beams, knees, mast step → caulk with oakum.

### Two orthogonal rulesets (the mechanic)
Gate each tradition by two toggles: **(A) shell-first vs frame-first** placement order, and **(B) clinker-overlap-rivets vs carvel-flush-treenails/M&T** joinery. Roman = shell-first + carvel-M&T; Viking = shell-first + clinker-rivets; English = frame-first + carvel-treenails. Same taxonomy, two switches → three mechanically distinct builders.

**Maps onto our existing model:** the native `Piece.type` already has `keel / rib / plank / deck`. The minimum viable distinction we can show *today* is **shell-first (planks before ribs) vs frame-first (ribs before planks)** — watch the hull assemble in the historically-correct order. Clinker-vs-carvel (overlapping vs flush strakes) and the full taxonomy (stem/sternpost/wales/floors/futtocks/keelson/knees) are later mesh + data upgrades.

## Open questions (not established by the research)
- **Strake counts & plank dimensions** per tradition (how many courses a player places for a Gokstad-class vs a Roman merchantman vs a 500-ton English ship) — needed to size the plank-by-plank loop.
- **Plank shaping** (spiling/lining-off, steaming/bending, tapering toward the ends) — does the player place pre-shaped planks or perform a shaping step?
- Detailed **Viking mast-step (kerling/mast-fish)** & crossbeam assembly, and the English **keelson/mast-step** assembly.
- Exact **carvel caulking recipe** (oakum is standard; the fuller oakum+tar+resin-in-the-rabbet claim was *refuted*, so don't model it as fact).

## Caveats
Strongest findings rest on peer-reviewed primary literature (Pomey/Kahanov/Rieth IJNA 2012; Kenchington, *The Northern Mariner* III:1 on Sutherland 1711 + the Dartmouth wreck). Viking findings lean on the Viking Ship Museum (Roskilde) + corroborating sites. **Killed by the adversarial pass:** (a) a strict "frames only after the first *five* strakes" rule — the sequence isn't strictly linear; (b) English carvel planking fastened *exclusively* with treenails — it used treenails **and** iron. Settled maritime archaeology; not time-sensitive.
